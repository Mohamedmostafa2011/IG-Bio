Auxin

The chemical messenger that tells plants how to bend, stretch, and find their way.

üß¨ What is Auxin?

Auxin (specifically Indole-3-Acetic Acid or IAA) is a powerful plant hormone produced primarily in the apical meristems‚Äîthe very tips of shoots and young leaves.

While animals have nervous systems to move, plants have hormones. Auxin is the master of cell elongation.

üî¨ The Micro-Science: The "Acid Growth" Hypothesis

How does a cell actually get longer? It's not magic; it's chemistry.

  1. Auxin triggers proton pumps in the cell membrane.
  2. These pumps push H+ ions (protons) into the cell wall, making it acidic.
  3. The acidity activates enzymes called Expansins.
  4. Expansins "loosen" the cellulose fibers of the cell wall.
  5. Water rushes in due to turgor pressure, and the cell stretches like a balloon!

Phototropism: Seeking the Light

Plants need light for photosynthesis. Phototropism is the directional growth response to a light source.

The Golden Rule: Auxin hates the light. It is photophobic.

1. Detection

Receptors in the tip (phototropins) detect blue light coming from one specific direction.

2. Redistribution

Auxin doesn't disappear; it moves laterally (sideways) to the shaded side of the stem.

3. Elongation

Higher auxin concentration on the shaded side causes those cells to pump acid and elongate rapidly.

4. The Bend

Since the shaded side is longer than the lit side, the stem acts like a bi-metallic strip and bends towards the light.

Gravitropism: Sensing Gravity

Even in total darkness, a plant knows which way is up. This is Gravitropism (or Geotropism).

üî¨ How do they "feel" gravity?

Plant cells contain specialized plastids called Statoliths. These are dense starch grains. Just like rocks sinking in water, gravity pulls these statoliths to the bottom of the cell. This physical settling signals the plant to move Auxin to the lower side.

Here is the critical twist: Roots and Shoots react differently to the SAME hormone!
