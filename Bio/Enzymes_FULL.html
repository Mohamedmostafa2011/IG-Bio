Enzymes

Enzymes are essential biological molecules. Without them, the chemical reactions in our bodies would happen too slowly to sustain life. This chapter covers their structure, mechanism of action, and the factors that affect their activity.

1. Definition and Nature

Definition: Enzymes are proteins that function as biological catalysts.

  • Catalyst: A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction and is not changed by the reaction (it can be used again).
  • Biological: Made by living cells (proteins).

2. The "Lock and Key" Mechanism

Enzymes are very specific. One enzyme only works on one specific substrate (reactant). This is due to the shape of the enzyme.

Key Terms:
  • Active Site: A specifically shaped area on the surface of the enzyme.
  • Substrate: The molecule that the enzyme reacts with.
  • Complementary: The shape of the substrate fits perfectly into the active site (like a key fits a lock).
  • Enzyme-Substrate Complex: The temporary structure formed when the substrate binds to the enzyme.
  • Product: The molecule(s) produced after the reaction. The product leaves the active site, leaving the enzyme free to work again.

3. Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

Enzymes are sensitive molecules. Their efficiency relies on the shape of their active site. If this shape changes, the enzyme stops working.

A. Temperature

1. Low Temperature: Reaction is slow. Molecules have low kinetic energy and collide infrequently.
2. Increasing Temperature: Rate increases. Kinetic energy increases, leading to more frequent and successful collisions between substrate and active site.
3. Optimum Temperature: The temperature at which the enzyme works fastest (usually 37°C in humans).
4. High Temperature (Above Optimum): The rate drops rapidly. The high heat energy breaks the bonds holding the enzyme together. The shape of the active site changes. The substrate no longer fits. The enzyme is Denatured.

B. pH

Most enzymes work best at a specific pH (Optimum pH). For most body enzymes, this is pH 7 (neutral). However:

  • Pepsin (in the stomach) works best at pH 2 (Acidic).
  • Trypsin (in the small intestine) works best at pH 8 (Alkaline).

If the pH becomes too acidic or too alkaline (too far from optimum), the enzyme becomes Denatured.

Exam Tip: Never say an enzyme "dies" or is "killed" by heat. Enzymes are molecules, not living organisms. Always use the term Denatured and mention that the "active site has changed shape."

5. Commercial Uses of Enzymes

  • Biological Washing Powders: Contain Proteases and Lipases to digest stains (food, blood, grass) at lower temperatures.
  • Pectinase: Used in fruit juice production. It breaks down pectin in cell walls, releasing more juice and making it clearer.
  • Lactase: Used to make lactose-free milk for people who are lactose intolerant.
