Diseases & Immunity

A disease is an abnormal condition affecting the body. Transmissible diseases are caused by pathogens (microorganisms that cause disease) and can be passed from one person to another. This chapter explores pathogen types, body defences, immunity, and antibiotics.

1. Pathogens

Key Definition: Pathogen
A disease-causing microorganism. The four main types are bacteria, viruses, fungi, and protoctists.

2. Transmission of Diseases

Pathogens are transmitted via specific routes:

  • Droplet Infection: Coughing, sneezing, or talking expels droplets containing pathogens (e.g. TB, Influenza).
  • Direct Contact: Touching contaminated surfaces or skin-to-skin contact (e.g. Athlete's foot).
  • Water: Ingesting contaminated water (e.g. Cholera).
  • Vectors: Animals that carry pathogens from one host to another (e.g. Female Anopheles mosquito for Malaria).

Case Study: Malaria

Caused by the protoctist Plasmodium. The mosquito acts as a vector (it is not the cause). It pierces the skin and injects saliva containing the pathogen directly into the blood. Prevented by mosquito nets, draining swamps, and insecticides.

Case Study: Cholera

Caused by the bacterium Vibrio cholerae. Ingested via dirty water. The bacteria attach to the intestine wall and secrete a toxin. This toxin causes chloride ions to be secreted into the gut, drawing water out of the blood by osmosis, leading to severe watery diarrhoea, dehydration, and potentially death. Treated with Oral Rehydration Therapy (ORT).

Exam Warning: Vector vs Cause
The mosquito is the vector for Malaria, not the cause. The cause is the protoctist Plasmodium. A vector is simply the organism that carries the pathogen from one host to another.

3. The Body's Defences

Physical & Chemical Barriers (Non-Specific)

  • Skin: Acts as a tough, waterproof barrier. Also secretes sebum (antimicrobial chemicals) and sweat (contains lysozymes that destroy bacterial cell walls).
  • Mucous Membranes: Line the respiratory and digestive tracts. Mucus traps pathogens.
  • Cilia: Tiny hair-like structures in the trachea and bronchi that sweep mucus up and out of the lungs.
  • Stomach Acid: Hydrochloric acid (very low pH) kills most ingested pathogens.

Phagocytes (White Blood Cells)

If pathogens breach the barriers, phagocytes carry out phagocytosis:

  1. Phagocyte detects chemicals released by the pathogen.
  2. It engulfs the pathogen by changing shape to surround it (forming a phagosome).
  3. Lysosomes (vesicles containing digestive enzymes) fuse with the phagosome.
  4. The enzymes break down and digest the pathogen.
Key Point: Phagocytosis is Non-Specific
Phagocytes attack any foreign organism they encounter. They do not distinguish between different types of pathogen — this is an innate, non-specific defence.

Blood Clotting

Prevents pathogens from entering through open wounds and stops blood loss:

  1. Platelets (cell fragments) are activated at the wound site.
  2. They release thromboplastin.
  3. Thromboplastin converts the soluble protein prothrombin into the enzyme thrombin.
  4. Thrombin converts soluble fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin.
  5. Fibrin forms a tangled mesh (net) over the wound, trapping red blood cells to form a scab.

4. The Immune System (Lymphocytes)

Key Definitions:
Antigen: A molecule on the surface of a pathogen that triggers an immune response.
Antibody: A specific Y-shaped protein produced by lymphocytes that binds to a specific antigen to destroy or neutralise it.

Lymphocytes are a second type of white blood cell. They recognise specific antigens:

  1. A lymphocyte encounters a foreign antigen on a pathogen.
  2. It starts to reproduce rapidly by mitosis to create identical clones.
  3. These clones produce massive amounts of specific antibodies shaped perfectly to fit that antigen (like a lock and key).
  4. Antibodies attach to antigens, causing pathogens to clump together (agglutination), making it easier for phagocytes to engulf them, or they neutralise toxins directly.

After the infection is cleared, some lymphocytes remain as memory cells. These can produce antibodies much faster if the same pathogen enters the body again, giving long-term immunity.

Exam Warning: Antigen vs Antibody
Do not confuse them! Antigen is on the pathogen (the lock). Antibody is made by the body's lymphocytes (the key). Each antibody is specific to ONE type of antigen. You cannot use the same antibody for different diseases.

Active = Your own body makes the antibodies (slow response, but long-lasting because memory cells are created).
Passive = You are given pre-made antibodies (immediate protection, but short-lived because no memory cells are created and the antibodies are eventually broken down).

6. Antibiotics & Antivirals

Key Definition: Antibiotics
Chemicals produced by fungi or bacteria that kill or inhibit the growth of bacteria (e.g. Penicillin destroys bacterial cell walls). They do NOT kill human cells because human cells do not have the same cell wall structures.
Huge Exam Trap: Viruses vs Bacteria
Antibiotics DO NOT work against viruses. Viruses have no cell walls, no ribosomes, and no independent metabolism — they replicate inside host cells. An antibiotic that targets bacterial structures cannot target a virus without killing the human host cell. Taking antibiotics for a viral cold is useless and drives antibiotic resistance.

Antibiotic Resistance

  • Bacteria can mutate randomly. Some mutations make them resistant to an antibiotic.
  • If a patient doesn't finish their antibiotic course, the weaker bacteria die but the resistant ones survive and reproduce (natural selection).
  • This creates "superbugs" like MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus), which are very difficult to treat.

7. Culturing Microorganisms (Practical)

Used to investigate the effect of disinfectants or antibiotics on bacterial growth:

  • Nutrient Agar: Contains carbohydrates, minerals, amino acids, and agar (a jelly derived from seaweed to solidify the medium).
  • Aseptic Technique: Sterilising equipment (Bunsen flame for the inoculating loop, autoclave for agar) to prevent contamination of the culture or the environment.
  • Method: The wire loop is dipped in bacterial culture, then streaked across the agar plate in a zigzag pattern. The lid is taped loosely (to allow oxygen in) but not fully sealed (to prevent dangerous anaerobic pathogens from growing).
  • Incubation: Plates are stored upside down (to stop condensation dripping onto the bacteria) at ~25°C in schools (safe temperature) or ~37°C in industry (matches human body temperature for faster growth, but requires stricter safety protocols).

Measuring Antibiotic Effectiveness

Small paper discs soaked in different antibiotics are placed on an agar plate that has been evenly spread with bacteria. After incubation, a clear zone (area of no bacterial growth) appears around each disc. The larger the clear zone, the more effective the antibiotic is against that bacterium.
