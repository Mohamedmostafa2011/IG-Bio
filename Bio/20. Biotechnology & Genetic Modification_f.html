Biotechnology & Genetic Modification

Biotechnology uses living organisms or their products to benefit humans. Genetic modification (GM) involves changing the DNA of an organism by inserting a gene from another organism. This chapter covers fermentation, GM techniques, applications, and ethical issues.

1. Biotechnology & Fermentation

What is Biotechnology?

Definition:
The use of living organisms (or their products) to produce useful substances or carry out industrial/medical processes. Examples include using bacteria to make insulin, yeast for bread/beer, and enzymes in washing powder.

Fermentation

Fermentation is the process by which microorganisms (mainly bacteria and yeast) break down substances in the absence of oxygen (anaerobic) or presence of oxygen (aerobic) to produce useful products.

Fermenters (Bioreactors)

Industrial fermenters are large vessels where fermentation occurs under controlled conditions to maximise product output.

  • Temperature control: Cooling water jacket prevents overheating (enzymes denature at high temps).
  • pH control: Maintains optimum pH for enzyme activity.
  • Stirrer/Paddles: Mix contents to ensure even distribution of nutrients and temperature.
  • Air supply: Provides O₂ for aerobic organisms; removed for anaerobic ones.
  • Nutrients: Carbon source (glucose), nitrogen source, minerals added continuously.
  • Aseptic conditions: Sterilisation prevents contamination by unwanted microorganisms.

2. Genetic Modification (GM)

Key Terms

Genetic Modification: The deliberate change of an organism's genome by inserting a gene from another organism (or modifying existing genes) using molecular tools.

Transgenic organism: An organism that contains a gene from a different species inserted into its genome.

Recombinant DNA: DNA that contains genetic material from two different organisms joined together.

Steps of Genetic Modification (General Process)

Step-by-step process:

1. Identify the desired gene in the donor organism (e.g. human insulin gene).
2. Cut out the gene using restriction enzymes (molecular scissors). These cut DNA at specific sequences, leaving "sticky ends".
3. Cut open the vector (usually a plasmid — a small circular piece of bacterial DNA) using the same restriction enzyme → matching sticky ends.
4. Insert the gene into the plasmid. Use ligase enzyme (molecular glue) to join the sticky ends → forms recombinant plasmid.
5. Insert the recombinant plasmid into a host cell (e.g. bacterium).
6. The host cell expresses the inserted gene, producing the desired protein.
7. Culture the bacteria in large fermenters to produce large amounts of the protein.
Exam Key Points:
• Restriction enzymes = cut DNA (scissors)
• Ligase = joins DNA (glue)
• Plasmid = vector (carrier)
• Sticky ends allow complementary base pairing to join the gene to the plasmid.

5. Cloning

What is Cloning?

Definition: The production of genetically identical copies of an organism. All cells in a clone have identical DNA.

Types of Cloning

Plant Cloning

  • Cuttings: A stem or leaf is cut and placed in soil/rooting powder → grows into genetically identical plant. Simple and cheap.
  • Tissue Culture: A small piece of plant tissue is placed in a growth medium with hormones → grows into many identical plants. Used to mass-produce rare/elite plants.

Animal Cloning

  • Embryo splitting: An embryo is split into multiple cells, each grown separately → identical offspring.
  • Nuclear Transfer (Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer - SCNT): Nucleus from a body cell is inserted into an enucleated (nucleus removed) egg cell → the egg is stimulated to divide → grows into a clone of the original animal. Example: Dolly the sheep.
Dolly the Sheep:
First mammal cloned from an adult body cell (1996). The nucleus from a mammary gland cell was inserted into an enucleated egg cell. Dolly was genetically identical to the donor sheep, not the egg cell donor.

6. PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

PCR is a technique used to amplify (copy) a small sample of DNA into millions of copies rapidly, without using living cells.

  • Uses DNA polymerase enzyme and primers.
  • Repeated cycles of heating (denature DNA) and cooling (copy DNA).
  • Uses: Forensic science (crime scene DNA), medical diagnosis, paternity tests, archaeological DNA, detecting GMOs.
