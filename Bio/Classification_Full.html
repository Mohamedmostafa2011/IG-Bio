Classification of Living Organisms

This comprehensive guide covers the variety of life, how organisms are named and grouped, and the detailed structural features used to distinguish the five kingdoms and their subdivisions.

1. Characteristics of Living Organisms

The concept of "life" is defined by seven distinct processes. In biology, if an entity does not perform all seven of these on its own, it is not considered living (this is why viruses are non-living). You can recall these using the mnemonic MRS GREN.

Definitions to Memorize:
  • Movement: An action by an organism or part of an organism causing a change of position or place. Animals move their whole bodies to find food; plants move leaves towards light.
  • Respiration: The chemical reactions in cells that break down nutrient molecules and release energy for metabolism. (Note: This is not the same as breathing).
  • Sensitivity: The ability to detect and respond to changes in the internal or external environment (stimuli).
  • Growth: A permanent increase in size and dry mass by an increase in cell number or cell size or both.
  • Reproduction: The processes that make more of the same kind of organism.
  • Excretion: Removal from organisms of the waste products of metabolism (toxic materials like Urea and CO2) and substances in excess of requirements.
  • Nutrition: Taking in of materials for energy, growth and development. Plants require light, carbon dioxide, water and ions. Animals need organic compounds.
Exam Tip: Be careful with Excretion vs Egestion. Excretion is metabolic waste (from cells). Egestion is the removal of undigested food (feces) through the anus.

2. Classification Systems

Classification is the process of sorting organisms into groups based on shared features. The largest group is the Kingdom, and the smallest is the Species.

Species: A group of organisms that can reproduce to produce fertile offspring. For example, a horse and a donkey can breed, but the offspring (mule) is infertile; therefore, they are different species.

The Binomial System

An internationally agreed system in which the scientific name of an organism is made up of two parts showing the genus and species. This avoids confusion caused by local common names.

  • The Genus is always Capitalized.
  • The species is always lowercase.
  • Example: Homo sapiens (Humans), Panthera leo (Lions).

DNA Sequencing

Traditional classification relied on morphology (shape) and anatomy. However, modern science uses DNA base sequences. Organisms that share a more recent ancestor have more similar DNA base sequences. This is the most accurate method of classification.

6. The Plant Kingdom

Ferns: Non-flowering plants. They have roots, stems, and leaves (fronds). They reproduce by spores released from the underside of the leaves.

Flowering Plants: Reproduce via seeds produced inside a fruit. Divided into Monocots and Dicots.

  • Monocots: Seeds have one cotyledon. Leaves have parallel veins. Roots are fibrous. Flower petals in multiples of 3. (e.g., Grass, Maize).
  • Dicots: Seeds have two cotyledons. Leaves have net-like veins. Roots have a main tap root. Flower petals in multiples of 4 or 5. (e.g., Rose, Bean).

7. Dichotomous Keys

A tool used to identify organisms based on visible traits. It consists of a series of paired statements (couplets). You observe the organism, select the statement that matches, and follow the instruction (e.g., "Go to question 2") until the organism is named.

8. Viruses

Viruses are not included in the 5 kingdoms because they are not living. They do not carry out the 7 life processes (like respiration) on their own. A virus consists of a strand of genetic material (DNA or RNA) surrounded by a protein coat. They are parasites that can only reproduce inside a host cell.
