Biological Molecules

All living organisms are composed of chemicals. This chapter covers the structure of carbohydrates, fats, and proteins, their component elements, and the practical chemical tests used to identify them.

1. Chemical Composition

Living matter is made of large molecules (polymers) built from smaller units (monomers).

2. Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the body's primary source of energy. They range from small sugars to large polysaccharides.

  • Monosaccharides: Simple sugars (e.g., Glucose).
  • Polysaccharides: Large molecules made of many glucose units joined together.
    • Starch: How plants store energy.
    • Glycogen: How animals store energy (in liver and muscles).
    • Cellulose: Structural component of plant cell walls.

3. Fats (Lipids)

Fats and oils. They are used for long-term energy storage, insulation (keeping the body warm), and making cell membranes. A fat molecule is not a polymer chain, but a unit made of Glycerol and three Fatty Acids.

4. Proteins

Proteins are essential for growth and repair. They are long chains of amino acids. The specific order of amino acids determines the shape of the protein, and the shape determines its function (e.g., Enzymes, Antibodies, Keratin).

5. DNA Structure

Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is the molecule of inheritance. It consists of two strands coiled together to form a double helix. Each strand contains chemicals called bases. Bonds between pairs of bases hold the strands together.

Base Pairing Rule:
  • A (Adenine) always pairs with T (Thymine).
  • C (Cytosine) always pairs with G (Guanine).

6. Water

Water is a crucial solvent in organisms. Most chemical reactions occur in water (in the cytoplasm). It allows for the transport of dissolved substances (digested food, urea, hormones) in blood and sap.
