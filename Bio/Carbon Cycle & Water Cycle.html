Material Cycling on Earth

Understanding how nature reuses elements to sustain life.

1. Introduction to Material Cycling 0:07-0:53

Life has existed for billions of years thanks to the Earth's ability to recycle essential elements like water, carbon dioxide, nitrogen, phosphorus, and carbon.

  • Continuous Reuse: Atoms within organisms are not new; they are continually reused.
  • Connection: The atoms currently in your body could have previously been part of ancient oceans, volcanoes, or other living organisms.

2. The Water Cycle 1:05-2:23

The water cycle describes the continuous movement of water through the environment.

Key Processes
  • Evaporation (1:20): Solar energy causes water to evaporate from lakes, oceans, rivers, soil, and puddles.
  • Transpiration (1:38): The specific evaporation of water from plant leaves.
  • Condensation (1:54): As water vapor rises and cools, it turns back into liquid to form clouds.
  • Precipitation (2:03): Water falls back to Earth as liquid (rain, snow, hail).

Outcome: Water seeps into the soil, flows into rivers, or is absorbed by plants to restart the cycle.

3. The Carbon Cycle 2:28-4:40

The carbon cycle is complex and involves the exchange of carbon between living organisms and the environment.

Main Carbon Stores (2:44)
  • Atmosphere: Stored as Carbon Dioxide (CO₂).
  • Biomass: Stored in plants and animals as biological molecules (e.g., glucose).
  • Soil: Containing microorganisms.
  • Fossil Fuels: Underground stores like coal, oil, and gas.
Movement of Carbon
  • Photosynthesis (3:18): Green plants/algae absorb CO₂ from the air to create biological molecules.
  • Consumption (3:39): Carbon is transferred when animals eat plants.
  • Respiration (3:35): Plants and animals release CO₂ back into the atmosphere to generate energy.
  • Decomposition (3:54): When organisms die, microorganisms (bacteria/fungi) break them down, releasing CO₂ via microbial respiration.
  • Fossilisation (4:13): Under anaerobic conditions (no oxygen), dead matter can slowly convert into fossil fuels over millions of years.
  • Combustion (4:28): Burning fossil fuels or plant matter releases stored carbon back into the atmosphere as CO₂.
