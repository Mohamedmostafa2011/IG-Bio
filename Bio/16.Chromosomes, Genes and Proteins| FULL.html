Chromosomes, Genes & Proteins

This chapter explores the molecular basis of inheritance. We look at what DNA is, how it is structured, and how the genetic code is translated into proteins that make up our bodies.

1. Chromosomes and DNA

Inside the nucleus of almost every cell in the human body, there are thread-like structures called chromosomes.

Key Definitions:

Chromosome: A thread-like structure of DNA, carrying genetic information in the form of genes.
Gene: A length of DNA that codes for a protein.
Allele: A version of a gene.

Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs) in diploid cells (body cells), but only 23 chromosomes in haploid cells (gametes: sperm and egg).

2. The Structure of DNA

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) carries the genetic code. Its shape is a Double Helix (like a twisted ladder).

Nucleotides

DNA is a polymer made of many monomer units called nucleotides. Each nucleotide consists of:

  • A Phosphate group
  • A Sugar molecule
  • A Base

Base Pairing Rules

There are four distinct bases in DNA. They always pair up in a specific way across the double helix, held together by hydrogen bonds:

Base 1Pairs WithBase 2
A (Adenine)↔T (Thymine)
C (Cytosine)↔G (Guanine)
Exam Tip: Complementary Pairing
Remember "Apple in the Tree" (A-T) and "Car in the Garage" (C-G). If an exam question gives you a strand A-C-C-T-G, the complementary strand is T-G-G-A-C.

3. Protein Synthesis

DNA codes for proteins. A protein is made of a chain of amino acids. The sequence of bases in a gene determines the sequence of amino acids in a protein.

The Process (Simplified for Core/Supplement)

  1. Location: DNA is too large to leave the nucleus. Proteins are made in the cytoplasm by ribosomes.
  2. Transcription (mRNA): A copy of the gene is made. This molecule is called mRNA (messenger RNA). mRNA is small enough to leave the nucleus.
  3. Translation: The mRNA travels to a ribosome.
  4. Assembly: The ribosome reads the base sequence on the mRNA and assembles amino acids in the correct order to form a specific protein.
ComponentRole in Protein Synthesis
DNAContains the master code. Remains in the nucleus.
mRNACarries the copy of the code from nucleus to ribosome.
Ribosome The site where proteins are assembled.
Amino AcidsThe building blocks joined together to make the protein.

4. Gene Expression

Every nucleus in your body contains all your genes (the entire genome). However, cells are specialized (e.g., nerve cells, muscle cells).

  • Specialization: Cells control which genes are used.
  • Gene Expression: In a muscle cell, the genes for muscle proteins are "switched on" (expressed), while genes for making eye pigment are "switched off".
  • This allows cells to perform specific functions despite having the same DNA.

5. Cell Division: Mitosis vs Meiosis

While usually covered in detail in Ch 17, it is vital to know the chromosomal difference:

  • Mitosis: Produces genetically identical cells for growth/repair. (Diploid $\to$ Diploid).
  • Meiosis: Produces genetically different gametes for reproduction. (Diploid $\to$ Haploid).
