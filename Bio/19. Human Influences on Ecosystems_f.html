Human Influences on Ecosystems

Humans affect ecosystems on a global scale through pollution, habitat destruction, over-exploitation of species, and introduction of non-native organisms. This chapter covers how humans damage ecosystems and what can be done to conserve them.

1. Food & Agriculture

Food Production

Modern farming intensifies food production to feed growing populations, but causes environmental damage:

  • Monoculture: Growing one crop over large areas — reduces biodiversity.
  • Pesticides: Kill pest species but also harm non-target organisms (e.g. bees, birds). Can accumulate through food chains (bioaccumulation).
  • Fertilisers: Artificial ones can leach into water bodies causing eutrophication.
  • Habitat destruction: Clearing land for farming destroys ecosystems and reduces biodiversity.

Eutrophication

Process of Eutrophication (must know in order):
1. Nitrates/Phosphates from fertilisers leach into a lake/river.
2. Algae grows rapidly → Algal bloom forms on surface.
3. Algae blocks sunlight → Aquatic plants die (can't photosynthesise).
4. Dead plants and algae decomposed by aerobic bacteria.
5. Bacteria uses up dissolved oxygen.
6. Fish and other aquatic animals die from lack of oxygen.

The Greenhouse Effect

Greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, water vapour) trap heat in Earth's atmosphere, like glass in a greenhouse. This is natural and essential for life. However, enhanced greenhouse effect from human activities causes global temperatures to rise.

Effects of Global Warming:
• Polar ice caps melt → Sea levels rise → Coastal flooding.
• Extreme weather events (storms, droughts, floods).
• Shift in biomes — species unable to adapt go extinct.
• Disruption of food chains and agriculture.

3. Deforestation

Large-scale cutting down of forests, especially tropical rainforests, for agriculture, timber, and urban expansion.

Effects of Deforestation

  • Biodiversity loss: Destroys habitats → extinction of many species.
  • Increased CO₂: Trees store carbon. When cut and burned, CO₂ is released → global warming.
  • Reduced water cycle: Trees return water to atmosphere by transpiration. Less trees → less rainfall → desertification.
  • Soil erosion: Tree roots hold soil. Without trees, rain washes away topsoil (leaching of minerals).
  • Flooding: Trees absorb rainwater. Without them, surface runoff increases → flooding.
Exam Tip: CO₂ from Deforestation
Two reasons CO₂ increases: (1) burning/decomposing trees releases CO₂, and (2) fewer trees means less CO₂ absorbed by photosynthesis.

4. Conservation & Biodiversity

Why Conserve Biodiversity?

  • Ecological: Stable ecosystems depend on many interacting species (food webs).
  • Economic: Species provide food, medicine (many drugs come from plants), materials.
  • Ethical: All species have a right to exist.
  • Scientific: Loss of species = loss of genetic information that could benefit future research.

5. Sustainable Development

Definition:
Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. It balances economic development, environmental protection, and social needs.
  • Renewable energy: Solar, wind, hydroelectric — reduces CO₂ emissions.
  • Sustainable fishing: Catching only what populations can replace. Use of quotas and mesh size laws.
  • Organic farming: Avoids synthetic pesticides and fertilisers to reduce pollution.
  • Recycling: Reduces resource extraction and landfill waste.

Sustainable Fishing

Overfishing depletes fish populations faster than they can reproduce, causing collapse of fish stocks (e.g. Atlantic cod). Measures to prevent this:

  • Quotas: Limits on how many fish can be caught.
  • Net mesh size: Larger meshes allow young/small fish to escape and reproduce.
  • Closed seasons: No fishing during breeding periods.
  • Fish farming (Aquaculture): Controlled breeding of fish — reduces pressure on wild stocks.

6. Non-indigenous Species (Invasive Species)

Introducing a species into an ecosystem where it didn't evolve can cause major ecological damage:

  • Compete with native species for food/habitat.
  • May have no natural predators → populations explode.
  • Can cause extinction of native species.
  • Example: Cane toads introduced in Australia; grey squirrels in the UK outcompeting red squirrels.
