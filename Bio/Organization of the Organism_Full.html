Organization of the Organism

This guide explores the fundamental unit of life: the cell. We will examine the structures within cells, the differences between plant and animal cells, specialized cells, and the hierarchy of biological organization.

1. Cell Structure

All living things are made of cells. Cells can be seen using a light microscope. While all cells share certain features, there are key differences between the cells of plants and animals.

StructureFound in Animals?Found in Plants?Function
NucleusYesYesControls cell activities; contains genetic material (DNA).
CytoplasmYesYesJelly-like substance where chemical reactions take place.
Cell MembraneYesYesControls what enters and leaves the cell (partially permeable).
MitochondriaYesYesSite of aerobic respiration (energy release).
RibosomesYesYesSite of protein synthesis.
Cell WallNoYesMade of cellulose; provides support and prevents bursting.
ChloroplastsNoYes (green parts)Contains chlorophyll; site of photosynthesis.
VacuoleSmall/TemporaryLarge/PermanentContains cell sap (sugar/salts); maintains cell pressure.
Important Distinctions:
  • Cell Wall vs. Membrane: The cell wall is fully permeable (lets everything through) and provides strength. The cell membrane is partially permeable (selective) and controls entry/exit.
  • Bacterial Cells: Bacteria are prokaryotes. They have a cell wall (peptidoglycan, not cellulose), cell membrane, and cytoplasm, but they lack a nucleus and mitochondria. Their DNA floats in the cytoplasm as a circular loop or plasmids.

2. Specialized Cells

In multicellular organisms, cells are adapted (specialized) to perform specific functions. This makes the organism more efficient.

Cell TypeFunctionAdaptation
Ciliated Cell Move mucus in trachea/bronchi Extensions of cytoplasm called cilia beat rhythmically to sweep mucus and dust away from lungs.
Root Hair Cell Absorb water & mineral ions Long projection increases surface area for maximum absorption. Thin walls for short diffusion distance.
Xylem Vessel Transport water & support Dead, hollow tubes with no end walls (continuous flow). Walls strengthened with Lignin.
Palisade Mesophyll Photosynthesis Packed with chloroplasts. Column-shaped and located at the top of leaves to catch light.
Nerve Cell (Neuron) Conduction of impulses Very long axon to carry signals. Myelin sheath insulates the signal for speed.
Red Blood Cell Transport Oxygen Biconcave shape (large surface area). Contains hemoglobin. No nucleus (more space for O2).
Sperm Cell Reproduction (Male gamete) Tail (flagellum) for swimming. Many mitochondria for energy. Acrosome (enzymes) in head to penetrate egg.
Egg Cell (Ovum) Reproduction (Female gamete) Large cytoplasm containing nutrient yolk. Haploid nucleus.

3. Levels of Organization

Multicellular organisms are organized in a hierarchy of complexity.

  • Cell: The basic functional unit of living organisms (e.g., Muscle cell).
  • Tissue: A group of cells with similar structures, working together to perform a shared function (e.g., Muscle tissue).
  • Organ: A structure made up of a group of tissues, working together to perform specific functions (e.g., The Heart - contains muscle, nerve, and blood tissue).
  • Organ System: A group of organs with related functions, working together to perform body functions (e.g., Circulatory system - Heart, blood vessels, blood).
  • Organism: Living thing performing all 7 life processes.

4. Size of Specimens (Magnification)

Microscopes magnify small objects. You must be able to calculate Actual size, Image size, or Magnification.

The Formula Triangle

I = A × M

I = Image Size (what you measure with a ruler)

A = Actual Size (the real size of the cell)

M = Magnification (how many times bigger)

Exam Tip: Unit Conversion
Always convert units to be the same before calculating. Usually, you convert millimeters (mm) to micrometers (μm).
1 mm = 1000 μm.
Example: If an image is 10mm wide and magnification is x1000, Actual size = 10mm / 1000 = 0.01mm (or 10μm).
