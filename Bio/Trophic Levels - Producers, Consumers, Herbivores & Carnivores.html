Trophic Levels in Food Chains

A visual breakdown of energy transfer, producers, and consumers in an ecosystem.

The Four Trophic Levels

Level 1: Producers 0:28
  • Organisms like plants or algae.
  • Produce their own food via photosynthesis using sunlight.
  • The origin of all energy in the food chain.
Level 2: Primary 0:49
  • The first consumers in the chain.
  • Known as Herbivores.
  • They exclusively eat plants and algae.
Level 3: Secondary 1:11
  • The second level of consumers.
  • Known as Carnivores.
  • Their diet consists of eating other animals.
Level 4: Tertiary 1:25
  • The third level of consumers.
  • Also carnivores, eating secondary consumers.
  • Often sits near the top of the chain.

Because of the massive energy loss described in the 10% rule, food chains typically have no more than four or five levels. There simply isn't enough energy left to support a fifth or sixth level population.

Special Ecological Roles

Apex Predators 2:36
  • Carnivores at the very top of the food chain.
  • They have no natural predators themselves.
Omnivores 2:49
  • Organisms (like bears) that eat both producers and animals.
  • Generally placed in Trophic Level 3, though their placement is complex.
Decomposers 3:17
  • Bacteria, fungi, and worms.
  • They break down dead plant and animal matter.
  • Crucial Role: Recycling nutrients back to the environment for producers.
