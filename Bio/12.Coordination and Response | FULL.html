Coordination & Response

Living organisms must be able to detect changes in their environment (stimuli) and respond to them. This coordination is carried out by the Nervous System (electrical) and the Endocrine System (chemical).

1. The Human Nervous System

The nervous system allows the body to react quickly to surroundings. It consists of the Central Nervous System (CNS) and the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS).

PartComponentsFunction
CNSBrain & Spinal CordCoordination center. Processes information and decides response.
PNSNervesConnects the CNS to every other part of the body.

Types of Neurons (Nerve Cells)

  • Sensory Neurons: Carry impulses from sense organs (receptors) to the CNS.
  • Relay Neurons: Found inside the CNS; connect sensory neurons to motor neurons.
  • Motor Neurons: Carry impulses from the CNS to effectors (muscles or glands).

2. The Reflex Arc

Reflex Action: A rapid, automatic (involuntary) response to a stimulus. It does not involve the conscious brain immediately. Used for protection.

The Pathway (The Arc):

  1. Stimulus: Change in environment (e.g., hot plate).
  2. Receptor: Detects stimulus (e.g., skin cells).
  3. Sensory Neuron: Electrical impulse travels to spinal cord.
  4. Synapse/Relay Neuron: Impulse crosses gap to relay neuron in CNS.
  5. Motor Neuron: Impulse travels out of CNS to the body.
  6. Effector: Muscle or Gland (e.g., arm muscle).
  7. Response: Action taken (e.g., muscle contracts, hand pulls away).
Synapses: The gap between two neurons is a Synapse. Electrical impulses cannot jump the gap. Instead, chemical neurotransmitters diffuse across the gap to trigger a new impulse in the next neuron.

3. Sense Organs: The Eye

The eye is a receptor organ that converts light energy into electrical impulses.

PartFunction
CorneaTransparent front layer. Refracts (bends) light.
IrisColored ring of muscle. Controls pupil size (light entering).
LensTransparent disc. Changes shape to focus light on retina.
RetinaContains light receptors (Rods - dim light; Cones - color).
Optic NerveCarries impulses to the brain.
Blind SpotWhere optic nerve leaves retina; no rods/cones here.

Accommodation (Focusing)

  • Distant Object: Ciliary muscles relax → Suspensory ligaments pull tight → Lens becomes thin (less refraction).
  • Near Object: Ciliary muscles contract → Suspensory ligaments slacken → Lens becomes fat/thick (more refraction).

Pupil Reflex

  • Bright Light: Circular muscles contract, Radial muscles relax → Pupil constricts (gets smaller).
  • Dim Light: Circular muscles relax, Radial muscles contract → Pupil dilates (gets wider).

4. The Endocrine System (Hormones)

Hormones are chemical messengers produced by glands, transported in blood plasma to target organs.

HormoneGlandFunction
AdrenalineAdrenal Gland"Fight or Flight". Increases heart rate, breathing rate, and pupil diameter. Converts glycogen to glucose.
InsulinPancreasLowers blood glucose. Causes liver to store glucose as glycogen.
TestosteroneTestesMale secondary sexual characteristics.
EstrogenOvariesFemale secondary sexual characteristics; menstrual cycle control.

5. Homeostasis

The maintenance of a constant internal environment (e.g., body temperature, water content, glucose levels).

Skin & Temperature Control

The skin helps maintain body temperature at 37°C.

  • When Hot:
    • Vasodilation: Arterioles near skin widen. More blood flows near surface. Heat lost by radiation.
    • Sweating: Sweat evaporates, removing latent heat from skin.
  • When Cold:
    • Vasoconstriction: Arterioles near skin narrow. Less blood flows near surface. Heat conserved.
    • Shivering: Muscles contract rapidly causing respiration (releases heat).

6. Tropic Responses in Plants

Plants respond to stimuli (light, gravity) via growth hormones called Auxins.

Phototropism: Response to Light.
Gravitropism: Response to Gravity.
  • Shoots (Phototropism): Positively phototropic (grow towards light). Auxin accumulates on the shaded side, causing cells to elongate faster there, bending the stem towards light.
  • Shoots (Gravitropism): Negatively gravitropic (grow away from gravity).
  • Roots (Gravitropism): Positively gravitropic (grow towards gravity).
