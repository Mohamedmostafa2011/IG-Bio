Movement In & Out of Cells

Substances move into and out of cells through diffusion, osmosis, and active transport. Understanding these mechanisms is crucial for explaining how organisms survive, exchange gases, and absorb nutrients.

1. Diffusion

Definition: Diffusion is the net movement of particles from a region of their higher concentration to a region of their lower concentration down a concentration gradient, as a result of their random movement.

Diffusion is a passive process, meaning it does not require energy from the cell. It happens because particles (gas or liquid) possess kinetic energy and move randomly.

Factors affecting the rate of diffusion:
  • Surface Area: The larger the surface area, the faster the diffusion.
  • Temperature: Higher temperature increases kinetic energy, making particles move faster, thus increasing the rate.
  • Concentration Gradient: The steeper the gradient (bigger difference), the faster the diffusion.
  • Diffusion Distance: The shorter the distance, the faster the rate.

2. Osmosis

Definition: Osmosis is the net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential (dilute solution) to a region of lower water potential (concentrated solution) through a partially permeable membrane.

Water Potential: Pure water has the highest possible water potential. Adding solutes (like sugar or salt) lowers the water potential.

Exam Tip: Terminology
Never say "Sugar moves." In Osmosis, ONLY water moves. Also, replace "concentration of water" with "Water Potential" to gain full marks.

3. Active Transport

Definition: The movement of particles through a cell membrane from a region of lower concentration to a region of higher concentration using energy from respiration.

Unlike Diffusion and Osmosis, this process goes against the concentration gradient. It requires specific carrier proteins in the cell membrane and energy (ATP).

Examples of Active Transport:
  • Root Hair Cells: Taking in mineral ions (magnesium, nitrates) from the soil, even though the concentration is higher inside the root than in the soil.
  • Villi (Small Intestine): Absorbing glucose into the blood when blood glucose levels are already high.
