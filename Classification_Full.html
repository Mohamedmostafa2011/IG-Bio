Classification of Living Organisms

1. Characteristics of Living Organisms

For an organism to be considered "alive", it must perform all seven of the following life processes. Viruses are not considered living because they cannot reproduce on their own or respire. The mnemonic to remember this is MRS GREN.

Exam Tip

You must memorize the specific definitions below. A common mistake is confusing "Respiration" with "Breathing". Breathing is a mechanical process; Respiration is a chemical reaction.

CharacteristicDefinition & Detail
Movement An action by an organism or part of an organism causing a change of position or place.
Example: Plants turn leaves towards light (phototropism); Animals use muscles to move.
Respiration The chemical reactions in cells that break down nutrient molecules and release energy for metabolism.
Equation: Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water + Energy
Sensitivity The ability to detect and respond to changes in the internal or external environment (stimuli).
Example: Skin detecting heat; Eyes detecting light.
Growth A permanent increase in size and dry mass by an increase in cell number or cell size or both.
Note: "Dry mass" is used because water content fluctuates.
Reproduction The processes that make more of the same kind of organism.
Asexual (1 parent, clones) or Sexual (2 parents, variation).
Excretion Removal from organisms of the waste products of metabolism (chemical reactions in cells including respiration), toxic materials, and substances in excess of requirements.
Examples: Urea (urine), Carbon Dioxide (lungs). This is different from Egestion (removal of undigested food).
Nutrition Taking in of materials for energy, growth and development.
Plants require light, CO2, water, and ions. Animals require organic compounds.

2. Classification Systems

Classification is the grouping of organisms. The ultimate unit of classification is the Species.

Definition of Species: A group of organisms that can reproduce to produce fertile offspring.

The Binomial System

Invented by Carl Linnaeus. Gives every organism two names.

  • First name (Genus): Capitalized.
  • Second name (species): Lowercase.
  • Format: Italics when typed, underlined when handwritten.
  • Example: Homo sapiens.

DNA vs Morphology

Modern classification is based on DNA Base Sequences. Organisms that share a more recent ancestor have more similar DNA base sequences. This is more accurate than traditional morphology (outward shape).

7. Viruses

Viruses are not cells. They are not living.

  • Structure: A protein coat (capsid) surrounding genetic material (DNA or RNA).
  • Why not living? Cannot reproduce on their own (parasitic). No respiration.
